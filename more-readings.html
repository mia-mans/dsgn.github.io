Readings

Ben Duvall: Selections from New Modernism(s)

This text uses Disney as an example to examine how aesthetics and various eras of modernism have developed overtime, as Disney's iconography has spanned decades. Disney is often criticized as a corporate supergiant, and as a results, its text style and icons are often reworked by designers to criticize capitalism.

Neta Bomani and Sabii Borno: Beyond Dark Matter

I was a big fan of this reading. Programming and CS in general are often posed as these exclusive, unattainable skills, and this reading does a great job of breaking down that invisible barrier. The tie between the selective memory of Black history and the way that computing forces us into thinking and acting a certain way is an innovative take that I think stands strong, and I appreciate this approach to thinking about programming.

Paul Soulellis: Performing the Feed

In this work, Soulellis argues that the post, a static, singular disruption that garners attention, has evolved into a smooth flowing feed, where everything passes endlessly and seems to blend into itself. He claims that automation and AI generated content have ruined the post, first through livestreams and eventually through artificial mindless, seemingly "off" content, but I beg to differ. While yes, this sector of unsettling artificial content has a strong internet presence, I don't think it has interrupted our daily lives enough to fully erase the post. (I got severely distracted reading through the James Bridle article, but I had never come across any of that kind of content on Youtube and the billions of views on those videos are often also automated). Even in the social media feeds that we endlessly scroll through, each post is unique enough and different from the next that I would argue it still constitutes what Soulellis considers the post.

Boris Groys: Art on the Internet

This reading poses the internet as having radically changed art, both in its presentation and in its production. I agree with Groys that virtual museum collections or photographs of art pieces act as documentation rather than the art piece itself and therefore change how viewers interact with the piece, but how does this apply to art made for the internet? Web art exists in a vastly different space than art created before the internet, and in the same way that an internet expression of a physical art piece is tampered with by online expression, web art loses some of its integrity when displayed as physical images of the page rather than in an online space.
