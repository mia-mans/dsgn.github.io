Readings

Ulises Carrion: Mail Art and the Big Monster

This text reminds me of the period during the pandemic when USPS was suffering and artists began using the mail more heavily to support them. I purchased postcards from artists who were charging no more than the postage cost and ended up finding pen pals online to send intricate letters to - I wonder if Carrion would have considered this more "Mail art"-y than the mail art he references? While both use the mail as simply a means of transport, the mail art that I interacted with was created solely with the purpose of using the postal system; an alternative method of transportation would have defeated the purpose of the art existing as it did. Similar to what Carrion says when discussing receiving something from a lover, the purpose of this art was in the 'mail' portion, not the 'art' portion.

Laurel Schwulst and Edouard U: How Do You Use the Internet Mindfully

My favorite website is the personal website of a post doc who I work with. It's built as a choose-your-own-adventure page with deliberate rendering of text. He told us that many of the faculty members disapprove of it, as it is inconvenient to click through the many paths to find his CV or wait for paragraphs of text to render. In that field, every person has one of three free CSS-templates as their website, and while yes, it is easy to find information because their websites are nearly identical, it also makes everyone extremely unmemorable from a quick google search.

I've always admired websites that have a dual purpose - that post doc's adventure game, a gsws thesis project posed as a murder mystery with hidden hyperlinks, a software engineer's homepage that has interactive fridge magnets, etc. Schwulst's quote that "Each artist should create their own space on the web, for a website is an individual act of collective ambition" speaks to the process of thinking of a website more creatively, but I believe that it should apply to everyone who uses the internet, not just those who describe themselves as artists.

Olia Lialina: STILL THERE

I appreciate Lialina's concept of 'ruins' vs moved pages vs 'alive' pages. In my mind, a website's status is based on its relevancy in todays world - Yahoo! is the most irrelevant search engine and I would almost consider it a ruin of the early 2000s, yet in the context of Geocities and Lialina's work in the archives, Yahoo! represents the future taking over as something even past an 'alive' page. I like the categorization of living pages as those whose contents all still work and links still exist; their functionality in our daily lives is insignificant compared to the importance of the content that they hold linking to a past era of the internet

Bojana Coklyat and Shannon Finnegan: Alt-Text as Poetry Workbook

The authors of this workbook strive to explain how and why to write alt text without writing a strict tutorial. They present ways to approach alt text more creatively, i.e. by using playful language reflective of poetry, while continually reminding readers that alt text is ultimately about increasing accessibility. Decolonising the Digital: A Personal Computer for Children of All Cultures

Language access in digital spaces, or lack thereof, speaks volumes to accessibility for various cultural communities and the preservation of cultural history as our world becomes increasingly digital. I researched this topic in depth in the context of 'Olelo Hawai'i (the Hawaiian language) in my first year writing seminar, and my takeaways from that project align heavily with Nasser's comments on internet accessibility for Arabic speakers: people from cultures with languages that do not have symbols from Western alphabets often cannot experience the internet in the same ways that we do.
